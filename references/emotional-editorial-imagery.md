# Emotional editorial imagery system

Use this system for cover, brand story, mission, vision, values, chapter transition, cultural narrative, and emotional synthesis pages. Do not apply it automatically to data, methodology, technical architecture, or evidence-heavy pages.

The reference evidence is the first seven pages of `CHANDON - BRAND EXECUTION GUIDELINES.pdf`. Learn the image grammar and composition logic; do not copy its photographs, logo, brand shapes, text, or exact page construction.

## Core principle

Treat photography as compositional material, not as an illustration placed beside text. Build an emotional sequence from several related but non-duplicative images, then bind them with one crop vocabulary, one tonal treatment, and one short verbal idea.

## Image selection bundle

For an emotional page, source a coherent bundle containing three or four roles:

1. **World** — landscape, architecture, travel, vineyard, coast, forest, street, or atmospheric interior.
2. **Human gesture** — candid adult interaction, hands, movement, shared activity, partial portrait, or an unposed social moment.
3. **Material detail** — fabric, skin, glass, stone, food, water, foliage, light, or another tactile close-up.
4. **Chromatic surprise** — one image carrying the accent hue or unusual light condition.

Favor globally oriented editorial lifestyle photography, but do not use ethnicity or nationality as the design rule. Select for mood, gesture, framing, and relevance.

## Photographic character

- Candid rather than corporate posing.
- Partial and close rather than fully explanatory.
- Natural light, backlight, dusk, haze, or reflected light.
- Slight grain and restrained contrast; avoid glossy HDR and generic stock brightness.
- Off-center subjects, foreground occlusion, movement, and unusual crops are welcome when the focal subject remains legible.
- Maintain one color temperature and comparable black level across a bundle.
- Never mix unrelated stock-photo aesthetics on one page.

## Seven composition patterns distilled from the reference

### E01 Integrated cover windows

Use one dominant lifestyle image plus two or three smaller landscape/detail windows that cut through the title field. Keep the title extremely short. The windows should feel embedded in the composition, not pasted cards.

### E02 Central portal

Use a full-bleed contextual image with a large neutral oval or softly sculpted portal holding a short section title. The portal is a deliberate negative-space device, not a button.

### E03 Founding-story editorial split

Use a narrow narrative column plus an asymmetric portrait cluster. Mix one larger historical or founder image with several smaller portraits. Align by optical mass, not equal card sizes.

### E04 Connected values windows

Use three overlapping or touching organic/arched windows, one per peer value. Put only one or two words on each image. Place the explanation below as a separate typographic line.

### E05 Sculptural personality objects

Use three isolated organic masks with generous space around them. Each image should express a distinct behavior through gesture, texture, or color. Avoid literal iconography.

### E06 Rhythmic mission gallery

Use five to eight related images cropped into a repeated arch, pill, or rounded window rhythm. Run one concise mission statement through the center as a horizontal verbal spine.

### E07 Vision diptych rhythm

Use two image rows with alternating arch directions or crop heights. Keep a central statement band. Use contrast between landscape, human action, and material detail to suggest possibility rather than explain it literally.

## Text-image relationship

- One headline or statement is enough; keep body copy outside imagery.
- Text placed on photographs is limited to one to three words per image window.
- Preserve clear native text objects; never bake text into generated images.
- Let the image cluster occupy 55-80% of the page on emotional layouts.
- Allow deliberate cropping and edge bleed, but keep faces, hands, products, and horizon lines intentionally framed.
- Avoid generic 50/50 image-and-text splits on emotional pages.

## Image sourcing SOP

Before search or generation, write an `imageIntent` with:

- narrative emotion;
- image role: world, gesture, material, or chromatic surprise;
- subject and action;
- lighting and time of day;
- camera distance and crop;
- intended mask and slide position;
- required negative space;
- palette and grain treatment;
- prohibited clichés.

Search or generate each role independently. Do not reuse the same image twice in a deck unless it is a deliberate narrative reprise.

## Integration with the structural system

- Google ROI logic remains authoritative for information hierarchy, grid, evidence, data pages, and pacing.
- This emotional system supplies image direction and artful crop composition for low-density narrative pages.
- Use emotional pages as a reset after two to four analytical pages or at a chapter opening.
- Target roughly 15-30% emotional-image pages in a normal mixed business deck. Increase only for brand/culture presentations.

## Forbidden shortcuts

- Generic smiling-office stock photography.
- A random foreign landscape unrelated to the message.
- Equal rectangular photo cards with captions.
- More than two photographic styles or color temperatures on one slide.
- Decorative photographs that compete with critical data.
- Copying CHANDON photographs, brand shapes, wordmarks, or proprietary layouts.
- Rasterizing a full page to preserve the collage.

## QA

Reject an emotional page when:

- the images do not share a recognizable mood or tonal treatment;
- the image choice is literal but emotionally empty;
- every crop has the same scale or centered subject;
- the image collage reads as a photo gallery rather than one composition;
- text is illegible over an image;
- an important face, hand, product, or horizon is accidentally cropped;
- the page contains facts or data better served by an analytical template.
